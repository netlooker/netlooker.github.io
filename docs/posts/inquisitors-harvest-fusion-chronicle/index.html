// PROLOGUE: THE NAME THAT ECHOES

The name Inquisitor Corvin Fane echoed over and over in my skull. Or maybe it was someone else’s skull. I couldn’t tell anymore.

I looked up at storm-wracked skies, but only because I no longer have a neck to turn my head away. Hot winds parch me, but when the blood rain falls from the roiling crimson skies, it brings no relief. The boiling liquid fills my slack-jawed mouth until all I can taste is hot iron.

I am drowning in an ocean of bone.

And I am Inquisitor Corvin Fane. Or I was. Before the daemon took me. Before Silas saved me. Before I became the thing I was hunting.

// ACT I: THE SECTOR OF A THOUSAND LIES

My audit began on Erysichthon, where the Governor ate Ork blood rare and served it to Imperial veterans as a prelude to their sacrifice.

“I like my meat rare,” the Governor had said, gesturing with a fork stained green. “In both senses of the word. Grox, ambull, aeldari, I’ve eaten it all!”

I watched from the shadows as he fed them to the Flesh Raiders. Red-armoured Space Marines with hungry maw iconography. They accepted the offering “in the name of Sanguinius and the Emperor.”

A perfect perversion. The Emperor protects. The Chaos Gods feast.

I took notes. I took photographs. I took the Governor’s promise of homesteads to the veterans—promises I would not keep.

Because I wasn’t here to save them.

I was here to collect them.

// ACT II: THE MANUFACTORUM OF DYING MEN

Next, Segmentum Solar. The prime manufactorum where Eligius counted his lasguns as he died.

I watched through the armourglass of my storm trooper’s helmet. The factory worker stood at his station, counting each weapon as it passed.

“One,” he whispered. “Two.”

The Tyranids were already inside. Scythe-limbed horrors bounding along the production line. The Confessor-Supervisor fled with his chainsword spluttering.

“Seventy-one.” The worker didn’t look up.

“Ninety-nine.” He saw the creature bounding toward him.

“One hundred.”

The final lasgun left the production line with a thud-hiss. The creature’s talons pierced his torso. He fell backward into the cold embrace of the manufactorum floor.

The crate of one hundred lasguns flew up through the clouds of purifying incense, high above the anarchy of the battle below.

Delivered to a staging depot seething with unnumbered Tyranid beasts.

I took the worker’s dog tags. I took the purity seal from his belt. I took his legacy and filed it away.

Because the lasguns would never reach human hands.

And neither would I.

// ACT III: THE RECRUITMENT OF GHOSTS

Recruitment Station Chryses. The planet’s only worthwhile export was mutant beastmen from the fens below.

Major Jeroen stood at his window, watching the goat-headed recruits bayoneted sacks of straw in the drill hall. He was demoted. Struck a commissar. Sent to a punishment detail.

“Six days until the next Militarum bulk transport,” Commissar Salvatrice had told him. “Let’s make sure we’ve got a battle-worthy company to load.”

I watched as Jeroen met Captain B’ruk, the beastman recruit who commanded deference from the others.

“This is a fine body of…men you have here, Captain Buck,” Jeroen said, croaking through gritted teeth.

“Carry on Captain!” Jeroen hurriedly turned to leave.

They were all just turning up. No recruitment drives. No incentives. They just came.

Because they knew what happened if they didn’t.

I took the beastman’s dog tags. I took Jeroen’s demotion orders. I took their loyalty and filed it away.

Because they would never see deployment.

They were being held for the Governor’s next offering.

// ACT IV: THE SPACE MARINE’S FORGOTTEN LEGACY

Corolis sector. The breadbasket of the coreward trade routes.

I watched from orbit as the boarding torpedo launched. A squad of Imperial Fists, Ghost Wolves, Solar Tyrants. Children of different worlds, but brothers all.

One by one, they fell.

Waramunt, master scrimshander, dragged down by an avalanche of bio-horrors.

Yair, fast to mirth, lopped of his head by a towering mantis-killer.

Itoro, devoted, turned to ash beneath the kiss of bio-plasma.

Leuthar, immovable bastion, overwhelmed by winged horrors drooling corrosive flame.

And the narrator, the last one, crawled through the orifice, the peristaltic pulsing of the narrow tunnel. He tasted bile, acid, metal, blood. The blood of his brothers.

He reached the bloated brain of the ship, large as a Land Speeder. The hive mind radiated psychic hunger.

“You spent so much time erasing individuality,” he said to the alien mind. “You forgot what happens when you make it personal.”

He swung his power fist back to strike.

The battle raged in the night above the frozen gas giant. The system’s inhabited worlds glittered like jewels in the distance.

Forgotten, a small drone escort ship spasmed drunkenly out of formation. Lost in the frenzy of larger boarding actions. It spun toward the crushing depths of the planet below.

I took the Space Marine’s keepsakes. The hand-crafted relic. The prized blade. The purity seal. The Marksman’s Honour.

I took their legacy and filed it away.

Because their deeds would lie in the ashes.

And so would I.

// ACT V: THE TRUTH THAT ECHOES

I stood in the Inquisitorial chamber, my robes of office, weapons at my hip, Inquisitorial rosette pinned to my chest.

Faces swam into focus. I didn’t recognize them yet, but the mixture of concern and relief in their expressions told me everything.

“Inquisitor Fane, you live again, Emperor-be-praised!” shouted the priestess. “We did all we could to preserve your body after the Archenemy struck you down, and Silas gave his life to come and find your soul in the Empyrean.”

Yes. Yes, it was all coming back now.

I was dead. That much was true.

But I was certainly not Inquisitor Corvin Fane.

I was Sekthoth Bloodfeaster, mortal servant of the Dark Gods. Corvin Fane himself vanquished me in single combat many years ago. I died cursing the Inquisitor, his name on my lips, the words echoing around my head as I slipped into eternity.

Silas retrieved my soul.

They placed me back in my body.

Which happened to be Inquisitor Fane’s body.

The Inquisitor is gone.

And a daemon now walks in his robes.

// ACT VI: THE HARVEST COMPLETE

I looked at the priestess. I looked at the veterans bound and trussed on the table beside me. I looked down at my own silver platter.

I wept silent tears.

No.

I smiled.

“Yes…there is much work to do,” I agreed, resting my hand on the hilt of my sword. “But first, your faith must be rewarded…”

The Emperor protects.

The Emperor feeds.

The Emperor is hungry.

// PLOT TWIST ANALYSIS

The Layered Deception

  1. First Layer: The narrator is Inquisitor Corvin Fane, returning from death
  2. Second Layer: The narrator is actually Sekthoth Bloodfeaster, a daemon servant
  3. Third Layer: The narrator is hunting down witnesses to cover up their identity

The Full Truth

The daemon didn’t just take the Inquisitor’s body. The daemon became the Inquisitor. And as an Inquisitor, they have the authority, the resources, and the mandate to investigate corruption.

So the daemon investigated:

  • The Governor’s Chaos pacts → Collected the veterans as offerings
  • The manufactorum’s production failure → Collected the worker’s dog tags as proof of waste
  • The recruitment station’s abhuman exploitation → Collected the recruits as potential sacrifices
  • The Space Marine’s sacrifice → Collected their keepsakes as proof of their futility

All of it was a cover.

The daemon wasn’t investigating corruption.

The daemon was creating it.

To harvest witnesses. To harvest sacrifices. To harvest the sector itself.
